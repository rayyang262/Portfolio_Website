ProcrastiNoMore

A new way to stay disciplined.

Background

This project investigates procrastination as a behavioral, cognitive, and emotional pattern that affects productivity, decision-making, and mental well-being, particularly among young people. Rather than treating procrastination as simple laziness, the work frames it as a response to overload, uncertainty, fatigue, and difficulty structuring complex tasks. Research indicates that a large proportion of students and workers regularly delay important work, often resulting in stress, guilt, and reduced performance.

The project explores how tools for task organization and time management—such as structured to-do systems—can either reduce procrastination or unintentionally reinforce it. By examining psychological causes, real-world habits, and existing productivity methods, the goal is to identify where current approaches fail and where design interventions can improve clarity, motivation, and follow-through.

User Research

User research investigated how people currently manage tasks, how effective those methods are, and where gaps remain in existing productivity tools. Key findings:

  • Over 80% of participants report regularly struggling with delaying work.
  • 67% already use some form of to-do list, yet only a portion consistently complete their tasks—current systems help with awareness but not follow-through.
  • The most requested feature is automatic schedule generation, followed by dynamic workload adjustment based on pace and capacity.
  • Users want task information integrated into everyday environments, not just a single app screen. Suggested locations included desks, kitchen tables, ceilings, and walls.

Interviews reinforce these patterns. A high-school student emphasized the need for fast, simple planning tools with reminders, goal tracking, and reward feedback. A graduate student balancing work and study highlighted the value of automatic planning, flexible workload adjustment, and convenient daily schedule visibility.

Existing productivity tools capture intentions but fail to convert them into sustained action. Users seek systems that actively guide planning, adapt to their pace, and remain visible in their daily environment.

Scenario Rendering

Desk Screen

Displays weather, idol poster, quotes, daily tasks, and monthly events. Useful for workers on breaks or checking upcoming items. With simple clicks, users see reminders and receive notifications automatically. For daily tasks, users tap to check off items and track progress so nothing is missed.

Ceiling Screen

Displays an overview of the day—whether it’s first thing in the morning or last thing at night, the user can check what they have completed and what they have for the next day. This helps the user feel accomplishment from completed tasks, and reminds them what’s coming so they stay disciplined and motivated.

Entrance Screen

Displays percentage data of work done by type, a motivational quote, prioritized tasks due, and tips on how to improve efficiency. When people come and go, they don’t stay at the door for long—this product shows important data at a glance, providing a quick reminder that keeps tasks front of mind.

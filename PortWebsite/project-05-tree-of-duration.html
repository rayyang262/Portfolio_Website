The Tree of Duration

An abstraction of time. A collaboration with Yelena Ye.

Concept

After reading Henri Bergson’s Matter and Memory, this project explores the philosophy that a viewer perceiving the world recreates, rather than receives it—through a tree installation.

The core philosophy: perception is not passive, but an act of selection—a freezing of the continuous flow of time into a stable image.

“An image may be without being perceived—it may be present without being represented… the distance between these two terms, presence and representation, seems just to measure the interval between matter itself and our conscious perception of matter.”
— Henri Bergson, Matter and Memory, p. 35
“To perceive consists in condensing enormous periods of an infinitely diluted existence into a few more differentiated moments of an intenser life, and in thus summing up a very long history. To perceive means to immobilize.”
— Henri Bergson, Matter and Memory, p. 208

Inspiration

The installation is designed as a space that warmly encompasses and gently hugs the audience—a tree with pixelated screens displaying different scenes. The screens show trees being cut down and turned into materials. Time determines its state; in each state, people perceive and remember it differently, embodying Bergson’s theory of perception.

Fabrication Design

After exploring inspirations, the design concluded on: a trunk that holds four screens. When the audience draws near, the four screens show the same stage (selected at random or played sequentially). As viewers approach, they hear only sound while the screens display randomized images of five different stages of a tree’s life.

Both solid and transparent textured outer shells were tested for the tree trunk, exploring how each option interacted with light and the overall aesthetic. The solid finish was chosen because it better concealed the Arduino layout and created a cleaner, more cohesive appearance.

Technical Details

  • Four screens mounted within a fabricated tree trunk structure
  • Arduino-controlled display sequencing for randomized or sequential stage playback
  • Proximity detection triggers audio playback as viewers approach
  • Five stages depict the lifecycle of a tree—from growth to material transformation
  • Audience interaction through spatial proximity rather than direct touch
